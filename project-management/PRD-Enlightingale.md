## 1. Vision

Enlightingale is a personal learning companion that closes the gap between curiosity and genuine understanding. It lets users create focused knowledge spaces — called **Muses** — around any area of interest. A built-in Research Agent does the work of finding, gathering, and organizing sources on your behalf. Then the app teaches that knowledge back to you through structured lessons, interactive quizzes, and a conversational voice agent you can interrupt and talk to like a real tutor.

The core loop is: **describe → research → understand → internalize.**

Enlightingale answers a question no other tool has solved: *what if you could go from curious about something to genuinely understanding it — and the app did the research and taught you, all in one place?*

---

## 2. Problem Statement

### The core experience

When someone becomes fascinated by a topic — a historical era, a scientific field, an emerging technology, a creative craft — they want to *genuinely understand* it. Not just skim the surface. Not just accumulate bookmarks. They want the kind of deep, internalized understanding that makes them able to talk about it, connect it to other ideas, and actually think with it.

Today, getting there is hard, slow, and lonely. The gap between "I'm curious about X" and "I actually understand X" is wide — and filled with friction that most people quietly give up on.

### What's broken

**Doing the research is a full-time job.**
To build real understanding, you need to find good sources, evaluate them, read them, and synthesize them. That process — before any learning even happens — is overwhelming. Most people never get past the first few tabs.

**Information doesn't become understanding on its own.**
Even if you collect great sources, reading them passively doesn't mean you've internalized them. There's no structure, no testing, no one pushing you to articulate what you've learned. The material sits in your notes app and fades.

**Generic learning tools aren't built around what *you* want to understand.**
Courses are too broad. Textbooks are too formal. Wikipedia gives you facts but not insight. None of these are built from your specific angle of interest, your prior knowledge, or the sources you actually care about.

**You can't have a real conversation with the topic.**
Existing AI tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity) can answer questions, but they don't know your specific research, don't build on accumulated context across sessions, and don't proactively teach. They're tools for lookup, not for learning.

### What Enlightingale resolves

Enlightingale collapses all of this into a single experience: describe what you want to understand, and the app does the research — then teaches you through structured lessons, quizzes, and a voice agent that you can interrupt and converse with. You can also enrich the knowledge base yourself. Everything stays grounded in your specific area of interest and your specific sources.

## 4. Core Concepts & Terminology

### 4.1 Muse

A **Muse** is the top-level unit in Enlightingale. It is a personal, focused knowledge space built around a single area of interest — e.g., "Byzantine History," "Quantum Computing Basics," "Italian Wines," "Urban Planning."

Each Muse contains:
- A **Resource Library** — all materials gathered by the Research Agent or added by the user
- A **Research Agent** — an autonomous AI that researches and builds the knowledge base on the user's behalf
- An **AI Knowledge Layer** — summaries, extracted concepts, cross-resource synthesis, auto-generated by the app
- A **Lesson Flow** — a structured, AI-generated curriculum that teaches the Muse content
- A **Visual Explorer** — an interactive way to search and browse the Muse visually
- A **Voice Agent** — a conversational AI that speaks to the user and teaches the Muse content

A Muse is deliberately personal and specific. You don't need to already know what to read or where to start — the Research Agent handles that. What makes a Muse yours is your area of interest, your questions, and the additional materials you choose to bring in.

### 4.2 Resources

Any piece of content that lives in a Muse:
- Web pages and articles gathered autonomously by the Research Agent
- PDFs, plain text, or Markdown files uploaded by the user
- User-written or voice-spoken annotations and notes
- Results from user-initiated in-app web searches
- Manual URL additions

Resources are the raw material from which everything else in the Muse is derived. They can come from the Research Agent, from the user, or from both working together.

**Voice annotations:** Users can speak a note, thought, or observation directly into the Muse. The voice input is transcribed, stored as a resource, and incorporated into the knowledge layer. This allows hands-free contribution while commuting, reading, or thinking aloud.

### 4.3 Research Agent

The Research Agent is Enlightingale's autonomous research capability — the feature that makes Enlightingale fundamentally different from any research or note-taking tool.

When a user creates a Muse, they provide a description of the area of interest and optionally their current knowledge level and what they specifically want to understand. The Research Agent then:

1. **Plans the research** — identifies the key subtopics, questions, and source types needed for comprehensive coverage
2. **Searches the internet** — runs targeted web searches across multiple angles of the topic
3. **Evaluates sources** — filters for quality, relevance, depth, and credibility
4. **Builds a curated Resource Library** — selects a set of sources that together give thorough coverage
5. **Reports back** — summarizes what it found, the structure of the knowledge it built, and any gaps it couldn't fill

The result: within minutes of naming an interest, the user has a rich, curated knowledge base ready to be taught — without having done any manual research.

**The user's role:** Users can review what the agent gathered, remove sources they don't want, add their own materials, and ask the agent to go deeper on specific subtopics. The agent can also run continuously in the background, updating the Muse as the topic evolves or as gaps are identified.

This is the starting gun for every Muse. It replaces the hardest part of self-directed learning: knowing where to begin.

### 4.4 Knowledge Layer

The AI-generated knowledge layer sits on top of raw resources. It includes:
- Per-resource summaries and key concept extractions
- Cross-resource synthesis (how different sources relate, agree, or contradict)
- A concept glossary for the Muse
- Gap analysis (what topics are underrepresented in the Muse)
- The structured content that feeds the Lesson Flow and Voice Agent

The Knowledge Layer is built from all resources — those gathered by the Research Agent and those added by the user.

### 4.5 Lesson Flow

A structured, page-by-page teaching experience generated from the Muse's knowledge layer. It functions as a mini-curriculum tailored to the user's specific area of interest:
- Narrative lessons that explain concepts clearly and engagingly
- Intriguing questions that prompt reflection and deepen thinking
- Short embedded quizzes to verify understanding
- Visual elements where appropriate (diagrams, data callouts)
- Progress tracking (which lessons have been completed)

The Lesson Flow adapts over time as new resources are added to the Muse — either by the user or by the Research Agent.

### 4.6 Voice Agent

A real-time conversational AI that teaches the Muse content via spoken audio:
- Reads and teaches from the Muse's knowledge layer
- The user can interrupt at any point to ask questions or redirect
- Maintains context within a session (remembers what was just discussed)
- Answers questions grounded in the Muse's resources
- Supports freeform conversation — not just scripted playback
- Adjustable tone and depth (introductory vs. expert level)

### 4.7 Visual Explorer

An interactive visual interface for exploring the Muse's content:
- Concept/knowledge graph showing how ideas in the Muse connect
- Visual search — find topics, resources, concepts spatially
- Entry point into lessons or deep-dives on specific nodes
- Highlights gaps and well-covered areas visually

## 6. User Flows

### 6.1 The Research Agent Builds Your Muse (Primary Flow)

```
User opens app for the first time
→ Clicks "New Muse"
→ Names it (e.g., "The Roman Republic")
→ Fills in: "What do you want to understand?"
   (e.g., "Why the Roman Republic collapsed and what made it so powerful before it did")
→ Optionally: "What do you already know?" (e.g., "I've seen the HBO show Rome, that's it")
→ Clicks "Start researching"
→ Research Agent begins: plans research, shows subtopics it will cover
→ Agent searches, evaluates, and gathers sources (1–3 minutes)
→ Agent reports back: "I found 14 sources covering X, Y, Z. Here's what I built."
→ User sees the Resource Library, browses what the agent gathered
→ User removes 2 sources they don't want, keeps the rest
→ Knowledge Layer auto-generates in the background
→ Lesson Flow appears: "Your Muse is ready to teach you"
→ User starts Lesson 1
```

### 6.2 Learning: From Lesson to Voice

```
User opens a Muse
→ Sees lesson progress: "3 of 8 lessons completed"
→ Reads Lesson 4 — a narrative about the Gracchi brothers
→ Hits a reflection prompt: "Why do you think land reform was so politically dangerous?"
→ Reads on, reaches end-of-lesson quiz — answers 3 questions
→ Switches to Voice Agent: "Talk me through what I just learned"
→ Voice Agent picks up, teaches the same content conversationally
→ User interrupts: "Wait — how does this connect to the fall of the Republic?"
→ Agent pivots, connects the dots using the Muse's knowledge layer
→ User: "Quiz me on this"
→ Agent quizzes verbally, gives feedback on answers
→ Session ends, summary displayed
```

### 6.3 Enriching the Muse with Your Own Input

```
User is reading an article on their phone (outside the app)
→ Remembers Enlightingale, opens the app
→ Pastes the article URL into the Muse
→ App ingests it, updates the Knowledge Layer
→ Lesson Flow adds a new section covering the article's content

— or —

User is thinking while commuting
→ Opens Muse, taps "Voice note"
→ Speaks: "I think the key thing I don't understand yet is why the Senate didn't reform earlier"
→ Note is transcribed and added as a user annotation resource
→ Knowledge Layer flags this as a gap, surfaces it in the next lesson
```

### 6.4 Ongoing Research Agent Loop

```
User hasn't visited the Muse in a week
→ Notification: "Your Research Agent found 3 new resources on the Roman Republic"
→ User opens Muse, reviews agent additions
→ Approves 2, removes 1
→ Knowledge Layer updates, new lesson content is appended
→ Voice Agent knows about the new material in the next session
```

### 7.1 Key Technology Decisions

| Component | Options | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontend** | React, Next.js, Svelte | React SPA — rich interaction, strong ecosystem |
| **Backend** | Node.js, Python (FastAPI) | Python FastAPI — strong AI/ML library support |
| **Vector DB** | ChromaDB, Pinecone, Weaviate | ChromaDB for v1 (local, simple); Pinecone for SaaS scale |

### 7.2 AI Architecture

**Research Agent Pipeline:**
1. User provides Muse topic description + knowledge level
2. AI generates a research plan: key subtopics, question angles, source types
3. Agent runs multiple targeted web searches across each subtopic
4. For each result: evaluate quality, relevance, depth → accept or reject
5. Accepted sources are fetched and ingested; summaries generated
6. Agent produces a coverage report + gap list
7. Runs async; status streamed to frontend; user can review on completion
8. Gap list fed back to trigger follow-up searches or Knowledge Layer gap analysis

**Lesson Flow Generation Pipeline:**
1. Resources ingested → chunked → embedded → stored in vector DB
2. AI extracts concepts, entities, glossary terms per resource
3. Cross-resource synthesis: clustering + summary generation
4. Lesson curriculum generated: topic sequence, narrative, quiz questions
5. Stored as structured JSON + Markdown; re-generated incrementally when resources change

**Voice Agent Pipeline:**
1. WebRTC audio stream from browser
2. Realtime API handles STT + LLM + TTS in a single low-latency loop
3. Muse knowledge layer injected as system context at session start
4. Barge-in detection handled at WebRTC / Realtime API level
5. Session transcript captured and stored post-call

**Voice Annotation Pipeline:**
1. User taps "Voice note" → short audio captured in browser
2. Whisper STT transcribes locally or via API
3. Transcript saved as a user annotation resource in the Muse
4. Knowledge Layer update triggered (async)

**Chat Assistant (RAG):**
1. User message → embed → retrieve top-k chunks from Muse vector DB
2. Retrieved context + user message → Claude claude-sonnet-4-6
3. Response streamed back with source citations
4. Web search augmentation when retrieval confidence is low

## 10. Non-Functional Requirements

| Area | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Latency** | Voice agent turn latency < 800ms (perceived); lesson page load < 2s |
| **Research Agent** | Initial Muse build (research + knowledge layer) completes within 3 minutes; status streamed live |
| **Ingestion** | Individual resource processing (summary + concepts) completes within 60s |
| **Scale (v1)** | Support up to 10 Muses; up to 100 resources per Muse; single user |
| **Reliability** | Voice sessions must recover gracefully from network interruptions |
| **Privacy** | In v1 (local), all data stays on user's machine. No telemetry by default |
| **Accessibility** | Voice agent naturally supports non-visual use. Text UI meets WCAG AA |
| **Responsiveness** | Web UI works on desktop (1280px+) and tablet (768px+) in v1 |

## 12. Open Questions

1. **Delivery model** — Local Docker app (privacy-first, v1) vs. SaaS (broader reach, recurring revenue)? Determines auth, storage, and pricing architecture from day one.
2. **Voice agent provider** — Claude Realtime API vs. ElevenLabs + Whisper? Realtime API is simplest; ElevenLabs gives more voice variety and potentially higher quality TTS.
3. **Research Agent autonomy level** — How much should the agent do without asking? Should it show its research plan and wait for approval, or just run and report back? Approval gate = more trust; auto-run = more magic.
4. **Lesson generation trigger** — Fully auto on ingest, or user-triggered? Auto is magical; triggered gives user control and reduces API cost.
5. **Visual Explorer priority** — This is a compelling differentiator. Should it be in MVP or v0.2?
6. **Monetization model** — If SaaS: per Muse, per seat, usage-based (by API tokens / Research Agent runs consumed), or flat subscription?
7. **Muse sharing** — Should a user be able to share a Muse read-only with a friend? Deferred for now but drives data model decisions early.
8. **Voice annotation privacy** — Audio is captured and transcribed. Should transcription be local (Whisper on-device) or via API? For the privacy-first v1, local is preferred.
